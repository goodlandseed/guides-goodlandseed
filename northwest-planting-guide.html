Where you're growing

Two climates, one Northwest.

Idaho's Treasure Valley and Oregon's Willamette Valley sit just a few hundred miles apart but behave like entirely different worlds for gardeners. Here's what you're working with.

Idaho
Nampa • Treasure Valley
Zone 6b–7a
Last spring frost ~May 1–15
First fall frost ~Oct 1–15
Growing season ~120–135 frost-free days
Summer temps 90–105°F peaks, dry heat
Annual rainfall ~11 inches — irrigate
The challenge: short season with intense heat. Start early indoors, transplant carefully after May 15, and choose fast-maturing varieties for anything that needs a long head start.
Oregon
Willamette Valley
Zone 8a–8b
Last spring frost ~Mar 1–Apr 1
First fall frost ~Oct 31–Nov 15
Growing season ~180–210 frost-free days
Summer temps 75–90°F, cooler nights
Rainfall pattern ~40 in/yr — wet spring, dry summer
The advantage: a long, mild season means more variety choices and multiple successions. Cooler nights love brassicas and beans. Start tomatoes in February and you'll have fruit by July.

Month by Month

Your planting calendar.

What to do when — side by side for Idaho and Oregon, from late winter through fall harvest.

February Late Winter
Idaho
  • Still too cold — rest, plan, and order seeds
Oregon
  • Start tomatoes indoors (8–10 weeks before last frost)
  • Start peppers and eggplant indoors
  • Order seeds & set up grow lights
March Early Spring
Idaho
  • Start tomatoes indoors (6–8 wks before May 15)
  • Start echinacea indoors — needs long lead time
  • Start basil, parsley, and slow herbs indoors
  • Prepare beds, amend soil
Oregon
  • Direct sow beets once soil hits 45°F+
  • Direct sow borage and calendula
  • Transplant tomato seedlings to larger pots
  • Sow cool-season greens & radishes outdoors
April Spring
Idaho
  • Direct sow beans when soil consistently 60°F+
  • Start morning glory indoors or wait for May
  • Harden off tomato seedlings mid-to-late April
  • Direct sow beets, spinach, chard early April
Oregon
  • Direct sow scarlet runner beans
  • Direct sow dragon tongue beans
  • Direct sow morning glory & nasturtium
  • Transplant brassica starts outdoors
May Late Spring
Idaho
  • Direct sow everything after May 15 (last frost)
  • Transplant tomatoes outside after May 15
  • Direct sow beans, squash, cucumbers
  • Direct sow borage, calendula, sunflowers
  • Transplant peppers with wall-o-waters
Oregon
  • Transplant tomatoes into ground (past frost risk)
  • Direct sow succession beans every 2–3 weeks
  • Direct sow summer squash and cucumbers
  • Transplant pepper & eggplant starts
  • Peak spring planting window
June Early Summer
Idaho
  • Direct sow morning glory now (soil is warm)
  • Succession sow beans every 3 weeks
  • Thin and stake tomatoes
  • Stay on irrigation — heat arrives fast
Oregon
  • Peak planting — almost everything goes in
  • Direct sow basil outdoors (nights warming)
  • Succession lettuce before heat sets in
  • Early tomatoes may be setting fruit
July Mid Summer
Idaho
  • Harvest calendula, beans, herbs regularly
  • Succession plant a final round of fast greens
  • Watch soil moisture daily — heat stress is real
  • Tomatoes need deep, consistent water
Oregon
  • Long harvest begins — tomatoes, beans, flowers
  • Scarlet runner beans producing heavily
  • Dry summer begins — start supplemental water
  • Succession sow fall brassicas indoors
August Late Summer
Idaho
  • Peak harvest — tomatoes, beans, flowers all at once
  • Direct sow fall beets now (harvest before Oct frost)
  • Save seeds from best performers
  • Watch for first cool nights — temps drop fast
Oregon
  • Harvest continues in full swing
  • Transplant fall brassica starts outdoors
  • Dry heat peak — deep water tomatoes
  • Start saving seed from top plants
September Early Fall
Idaho
  • Race to harvest before first frost (Oct 1–15)
  • Pick tomatoes early — ripen indoors if needed
  • Harvest and dry all herb seed heads
  • Dig & cure winter squash
Oregon
  • Harvest continues — long fall ahead
  • Tomatoes still producing, hold on the vine
  • Fall greens and root crops coming in
  • Collect seed from dry pods and flowers
October Fall
Idaho
  • First frost arrives Oct 1–15 — harvest ALL
  • Pull tomatoes, peppers, remaining squash
  • Cover beets to extend a week or two
  • Clean up beds, add compost
Oregon
  • Harvest continues — many things still producing
  • First frost arrives late October (sometimes Nov)
  • Harvest last tomatoes, peppers before frost
  • Fall greens thriving in cool temps
November Late Fall
Idaho
  • Garden to bed — mulch perennials
  • Clean, label, and store saved seeds
  • Plan next year's rotation
Oregon
  • Put summer garden to bed
  • Winter crops (kale, chard) still producing
  • Clean and store saved seed
  • Lemon balm & echinacea may look dead — they're not

What to grow

Variety recommendations.

These are the varieties we grow, sell, and trust in each climate. Not guesswork — actual results from our farm and our customers.

Idaho Favorites

Treasure Valley — Zone 6b–7a, short season, dry heat

Black Krim Tomato
Handles the Treasure Valley heat better than most heirlooms. Rich, smoky flavor. Start indoors in March, plant out after May 15.
Dragon Tongue Bean
Fast to harvest (50–55 days) — perfect for Idaho's shorter window. Yellow with purple streaks, prolific producer, and it doesn't mind the heat.
Chioggia Beet
Tolerates cool soil better than most beets — direct sow early or late summer for fall harvest. Beautiful candy-striped interior.
Calendula (Resina or Flashback)
Thrives in dry Idaho summers. Resina for high resin medicinal use, Flashback for mixed colors. Both are incredibly prolific from June on.
Borage
A heat lover that pollinators adore. Self-seeds reliably in Idaho — plant once and it returns. Great companion near tomatoes and beans.
Anise Hyssop
Drought tolerant and fragrant. Happy in the heat. Once established, it shrugs off Idaho dry spells and flowers for months. Bee magnet.

Oregon Favorites

Willamette Valley — Zone 8a–8b, long season, wet springs

Brandywine Tomato
Needs 80+ days and a long season — Oregon's climate was made for this variety. Start February indoors, enjoy August and September harvests.
Cherokee Purple Tomato
Loves Oregon's longer days and warmer nights that come slowly. Deep burgundy-purple fruit with complex flavor. Start February, harvest August.
Scarlet Runner Bean
Loves cool Oregon nights and becomes the star of the summer garden. Edible pods, stunning red flowers, and hummingbirds can't stay away.
Echinacea
The wet-dry cycle of the Willamette Valley mirrors echinacea's native prairie. It may overwinter as a perennial here — worth the long-game investment.
Lemon Balm
A true perennial in Zone 8. Plant once and it returns each spring, spreading gently. Wonderful for tea, tinctures, and keeping pollinators around.
Morning Glory (Grandpa Ott's)
The longer days of a Pacific Northwest summer are exactly what morning glory needs to put on its full show. Direct sow in April; blooms by July.

Crossing the Cascades

Moving from Idaho to Oregon?

We made this move ourselves — and it changes more than just your frost dates. Here's what carries over and what you'll need to relearn.

What travels well

  • Your seed collection — almost everything you grew in Idaho grows in Oregon too, just with a longer window and more abundance
  • Compost habits — keep building soil biology, it matters just as much in the Willamette
  • Borage and calendula — both thrive in either climate; they'll self-seed just as readily in Oregon
  • Beet varieties — your Chioggia and Detroit Red do great; direct sow even earlier in Oregon
  • Your sense of timing — you're already used to planning ahead; just shift the calendar back by 4–6 weeks

What changes

  • Start tomatoes in February, not March — you have the runway, use it. Brandywine and Cherokee Purple become achievable.
  • Less watering in spring — Oregon's wet season does the work until June. Adjust irrigation habits for the dry summer instead.
  • Lemon balm and echinacea may overwinter as perennials — plant them expecting they might stay
  • Scarlet runner beans become a garden centerpiece, not a risk — they need the cooler nights that Oregon reliably provides
  • Your frost window expands 6–8 weeks on each end — that's a full extra month of growing on both sides of the season
  • Mildew and fungal pressure increases in wet springs — space plants generously and water at soil level
